# Notes from a Year of Running

I took up running last spring, and a year on I want to set down what I learned. The first lesson was humbling. I could barely manage ten minutes without stopping, lungs burning, legs heavy, certain I had made a mistake. But I went out again two days later, and then again, and slowly the ten minutes became twenty.

Consistency turned out to matter far more than intensity. The runners I admired were not the fastest — they were the ones who simply kept showing up. Three short runs a week, held to without fail, beat one heroic effort followed by a fortnight on the sofa. The body adapts to what you ask of it regularly, not to what you ask of it once.

Pace was the next thing I had wrong. I ran too hard, too soon, every single time. A wiser friend told me to slow down until I could hold a conversation, and the advice transformed everything. Easy running built the base that let the occasional hard run actually count. Speed, it turns out, is built on a foundation of slowness.

The weather stopped being an excuse once I bought the right clothes. Rain is fine with a decent jacket. Cold is fine once you are moving. The hardest step was always the front door, and almost never the run itself. On the mornings I least wanted to go, I felt best afterwards, without exception.

Injuries taught me respect. A twinge ignored becomes a layoff, and a layoff undoes weeks of patient work. Rest days are part of training, not a failure of it. I learned to read the small signals and to ease off before they grew loud. The goal, after all, is to still be running next year, and the year after that.

A year in, the numbers have improved, but that is not really the point. Running cleared my head on hard days and gave a shape to my mornings. It taught me that large things are built from small, repeated acts. I am not fast, and I may never be, yet I am a runner now, and that quiet fact still surprises me.
