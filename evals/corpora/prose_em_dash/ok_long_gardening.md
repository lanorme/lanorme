# Planning a Small Kitchen Garden

A kitchen garden need not be large to be rewarding. A few square metres, given sun and decent soil, can supply a household with salad, herbs, and a steady trickle of vegetables through the warmer months. The trick is to plan with the space you have rather than the space you wish you had. Modest ambitions, well executed, beat grand ones that go to seed.

Start by watching where the sun falls. Most vegetables want at least six hours of direct light a day. Note which corners stay shaded and which bake in the afternoon. Leafy greens tolerate some shade, while tomatoes and peppers demand the brightest spot you can offer. Matching each plant to its place is half the work of a good harvest.

Soil comes next, and here patience pays. Dig in well rotted compost before you plant anything. Good soil holds moisture without turning to mud and feeds your crops slowly through the season. If your ground is heavy clay or thin sand, raised beds filled with a decent mix will save you years of frustration. The bed you build once serves you for many summers.

Choose crops you will actually eat. It sounds obvious, yet many beginners plant rows of things that end up composted untouched. Grow the herbs you reach for in the kitchen — basil, parsley, thyme, and mint in a pot of its own, since it spreads with abandon. A short row of lettuce sown every fortnight gives you leaves all summer without a glut.

Watering is the daily rhythm of the garden. Water deeply and less often rather than a little every day, so roots learn to reach down. Early morning is best, before the heat draws the moisture off. A layer of mulch keeps the soil cool and cuts the work considerably. The garden asks for attention, but it asks gently, and it gives far more than it takes.

By late summer the rewards arrive in earnest. There is a particular satisfaction in eating something you grew yourself, picked minutes before the meal. It tastes of effort and weather and care. Even a small plot teaches patience, attention, and a quiet respect for the seasons. That lesson, more than the produce, may be the truest harvest of all.
